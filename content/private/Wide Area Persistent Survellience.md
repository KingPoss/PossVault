Wide Area Motion Imagery, otherwise known as Wide Area Persistent Surveillance, is an intelligence-gathering method that involves using arrays of cameras (usually from manned planes or UAVs) to collect video footage of a given area. This footage is then stitched together into one large, high resolution video. Its deployed for long periods of time, allowing chronologically ordered surveillance on the movements of people and vehicles.

Combined with automatic imagery analysis, like DARPA'S Persistent Stare Exploitation and Analysis System (PerSEAS), these systems can "detect indicators of suspicious behavior" by analyzing the massive swathes of data collected, to find strange patterns. In example, two cars, meeting in an area off a main road, over multiple times in a week/month. 

Police departments all over the U.S. are using private companies to implement persistent surveillance. This has been occurring at least as far back as 2007.
